Part 2 — The Strategic Thesis

The core thesis

I build managed AI workflow systems for federal contractors and document-heavy operations.

The simpler sentence:

I help small and mid-sized federal contractors and document-heavy teams turn manual proposal, compliance, intake, review, and knowledge workflows into managed AI-assisted internal tools.

The business is not “AI consulting.” It is not “RFP SaaS.” It is not “chatbots for businesses.” It is a workflow engineering practice.

The buyer’s pain sounds like:

* “Our proposal team is drowning.”
* “We manually read 100–300 page RFPs.”
* “Our compliance matrix process is slow and error-prone.”
* “Our past performance and resumes are scattered.”
* “Our contract mods and SOWs get reviewed manually.”
* “Our knowledge lives in SharePoint and nobody can find anything.”
* “We are too small for a giant enterprise platform, but too sophisticated for spreadsheets.”
* “We do not need an AI strategy deck. We need one workflow fixed.”

The business exists in the gap between generic SaaS tools and messy real-world operations.

The important nuance

This business should not compete head-on with GovCon proposal SaaS.

The pitch is not:

“I built a better RFP tool than GovDash, Procurement Sciences, Sweetspot, Loopio, Responsive, AutoGenAI, or every other proposal platform.”

The pitch is:

“I build managed AI workflow systems around the document-heavy parts of your operation that your existing tools do not fully solve.”

SaaS is part of the environment. Sometimes SaaS is the answer. Sometimes a custom workbench is the answer. Sometimes the real answer is a thin integration layer between email, SharePoint, PDFs, spreadsheets, a CRM, and human review. The Teardown decides which.

The core opportunity

Small and mid-sized federal contractors often have enough document volume to feel pain, but not enough internal AI/product engineering talent to build custom systems. Many are too small for enterprise software rollouts but too mature to keep running core workflows through inboxes, spreadsheets, shared drives, and manual review.

This is the wedge:

Too document-heavy for manual work. Too specific for generic SaaS. Too small for enterprise transformation. Big enough to pay for a managed workflow system.

Part 4 — What I Actually Build

The deliverable is usually not a report, CLI, desktop app, mobile app, or generic chatbot.

The deliverable is usually:

A private internal AI workbench — a web app that wraps AI around one painful workflow while keeping humans in control.

A typical workbench does this:

1. User uploads, forwards, or imports documents/emails/forms.
2. The system classifies the document or request.
3. The system extracts important fields.
4. The system flags missing, uncertain, or risky items.
5. The human reviews and edits the output.
6. The system drafts the next step: email, summary, checklist, compliance matrix, internal note, or response outline.
7. The human approves.
8. The output is exported, copied, saved, emailed, or pushed into the next system.
9. The app logs what happened for auditability and future improvement.

The goal is not “replace people.” The goal is:

AI does the repetitive reading, extracting, checking, and drafting. Humans keep judgment, approval, and accountability.

Pattern 1 — Document intake and extraction pipeline

Best for workflows where documents arrive by email, upload, portal, shared drive, or form.

Common features:

* Upload or email intake
* Document classification
* AI extraction of structured fields
* Missing-info detection
* Confidence scores
* Source citations / page references
* Human review and correction
* Status queue
* Export to CSV/PDF/JSON
* Audit log

Example use cases:

* RFP intake packet parsing
* Subcontractor document intake
* Contract modification review intake
* Insurance document intake
* Title/closing packet review
* Accounting document collection

Pattern 2 — Proposal / compliance workbench

Best for federal contractors with RFP and compliance workflows.

Common features:

* RFP upload or SAM.gov/opportunity ingestion
* Requirement extraction
* Section L/M/C identification
* Compliance matrix generation
* FAR/clause extraction
* Q&A/amendment tracking
* Past performance matching
* Resume/capability matching
* Proposal outline generation
* Risk/missing-requirement flags
* Human review queue
* Export to Word/Excel/PDF

This is where OmniBid is most directly useful as a demo and starting architecture.

Pattern 3 — Internal knowledge agent / RAG workbench

Best for teams where knowledge is scattered across SharePoint, Google Drive, SOPs, past proposals, policies, templates, contracts, and old files.

Common features:

* Document ingestion
* Permission-aware search where required
* Cited answers
* Source links
* Saved answers/prompts
* Admin document management
* Usage analytics
* Escalation to human owner

The key is not “chat with docs.” The key is:

Reduce time spent hunting for the right answer, template, clause, past response, or internal policy.

Pattern 4 — AI drafting workbench

Best when the final output is written work that humans currently draft from repeated context.

Common features:

* Structured input form
* Retrieval from approved source material
* Draft generation
* Human edit/approve
* Version history
* Export to Word/PDF/email
* Tone/style templates
* Compliance checklist against source requirements

Example outputs:

* Proposal response outlines
* Follow-up emails
* Executive summaries
* Internal review notes
* Compliance summaries
* Customer/vendor replies

Pattern 5 — Workflow router / triage dashboard

Best when inbound requests need to be classified, assigned, and tracked.

Common features:

* Email/folder/form ingestion
* Request classification
* Priority scoring
* Metadata extraction
* Owner assignment
* SLA/status dashboard
* Notifications
* Draft next action
* Manager visibility

This is especially useful when the pain is not one document but the constant flow of incoming items.

Part 6 — The Offer

One front-door offer. One main build offer. One required launch-support layer. Optional maintenance or growth retainer after launch.

Front door — AI Workflow Teardown

Founding price: $5,000 for the first 2–3 serious founding engagements.

Standard price: $7,500–$10,000 once proof assets exist.

The promise:

I map one expensive document-heavy workflow, prototype the AI-assisted version, and give you a fixed-price build plan.

The Teardown is not an open-ended audit. It is one workflow, one prototype, one build plan.

Teardown deliverables

1. Current-state workflow map — how the workflow actually happens today, including triggers, systems, handoffs, documents, people, time/cost assumptions, errors, and bottlenecks.
2. Target-state AI workflow — what the workflow looks like with AI assistance, where humans stay in the loop, where data flows, and what outputs are produced.
3. Working proof-of-concept — a rough but live prototype of the highest-ROI slice of the workflow.
4. ROI estimate — labor savings, cycle-time reduction, error reduction, or revenue/capacity impact, with explicit assumptions.
5. Risk/security review — what data goes where, what models are used, what is human-reviewed, what is logged, what should not be automated.
6. Buy/build/integrate recommendation — whether to use existing SaaS, custom build, or a hybrid integration layer.
7. Production architecture — the stack and components recommended for the production version.
8. Fixed-price build options — usually Lite / Standard / Advanced or one recommended build with optional add-ons.

Founding engagement rule

The first 2–3 founding engagements can be discounted to $5,000 in exchange for:

* fast access to workflow owners
* real sample documents where allowed
* permission to create an anonymized or named case study if successful
* testimonial request if the client is satisfied
* honest feedback on the deliverable

A free pilot is allowed only when the proof value is unusually high: strong logo, warm relationship, highly relevant workflow, strong testimonial potential, or strategic referral value.

Credit mechanic

100% of the Teardown fee is credited toward a Production Build signed within 14 days of Teardown delivery.

This creates urgency while making the Teardown feel like a serious decision artifact rather than a sunk cost.

Part 7 — Managed Hosting, Infrastructure, and Support Model

Default early model

For early and smaller clients, the default offer is managed:

* I host the app.
* I manage deployment.
* I manage backend/database/file storage.
* I manage AI keys and usage tracking.
* I monitor errors and performance.
* The client gets private login and a working tool.

This reduces friction. Most small and mid-sized firms do not want to manage infrastructure.

Required boundaries

Managed does not mean unlimited liability or 24/7 operations.

Every contract must define:

* support hours
* response windows
* whether 24/7 support is included, usually not
* uptime target, if any
* what counts as a bug
* what counts as a feature request
* what counts as out-of-scope
* AI usage cap
* data retention policy
* backup/export policy
* cancellation policy
* migration/handoff terms
* human-review requirement
* decision-support framing
* liability limits

Client-owned infrastructure option

For larger or security-sensitive clients, offer a client-owned infrastructure option.

Possible structure:

* Client owns Vercel/AWS/Azure/GCP/Convex/Supabase accounts.
* Client owns AI API keys.
* Client owns file storage.
* I deploy and maintain with contractor access.
* Implementation price is higher because setup and governance take longer.
* Retainer may be lower operationally, but support remains defined.

This turns security concerns into a choice instead of a blocker.

CUI / CMMC deployment tiers

Compliance posture across the GovCon ICP varies widely. "Federal contractor" covers a janitorial firm on a civilian agency contract with zero CUI exposure all the way through a defense engineering contractor that handles CUI as a baseline assumption. Defaulting every GovCon engagement to GovCloud prices out half the ICP unnecessarily — GovCloud setup adds 2–3 weeks and meaningful cost to a build. Defaulting every GovCon engagement to commercial managed hosting creates a compliance violation for the buyer the moment CUI lands on the stack, and the buyer's compliance officer will kill the deal silently before the second call. The fix is not "default to GovCloud." It is "qualify CUI exposure during discovery and route to the appropriate deployment model."

The discovery call qualifying question (also added to Part 12) routes the engagement:

"Do any of the documents in this workflow contain Controlled Unclassified Information, or material covered by DFARS 252.204-7012, ITAR, or your prime's CMMC flowdown requirements?"

Three deployment tiers follow from the answer.

Tier 1 — No CUI exposure

For workflows handling pre-award marketing materials, public RFP responses, capability statements without sensitive past performance, and internal knowledge with no controlled data. Most small federal contractors' proposal intake workflows are actually in this bucket — they process public SAM.gov solicitations, not classified specs.

* Default managed hosting on my stack
* SOW explicitly limits scope to non-CUI documents
* Client is contractually responsible for not uploading CUI to the system
* Standard pricing

Tier 2 — CUI exposure, client lacks GovCloud / Azure Government

Realistic case for many small contractors who have not built up to CMMC Level 2 yet but whose pipeline includes some controlled data.

* Deploy to client-owned commercial infrastructure with CUI exclusion in the SOW, or
* Scope the workflow to the non-CUI portions of their pipeline
* Pricing reflects added setup and governance work
* The scoping conversation happens during the Teardown, not after the Build is signed

Tier 3 — CUI must be handled on compliant infrastructure

For contractors with established GovCloud or Azure Government tenants whose workflow must operate on CUI.

* Deploy to client-owned GovCloud or Azure Government
* Implementation price is meaningfully higher — GovCloud configuration is real work and the engagement takes on contractor responsibilities inside the client's compliance boundary
* Screen aggressively for fit; CMMC-adjacent work carries audit trail and incident response obligations a solo operator may not be ready to take on
* Offered only when the client owns their own compliance boundary and is buying me as an implementer, not as a vendor inside their CMMC flowdown chain

Year 1 boundary

Engagements that require formal CMMC vendor-flowdown certification (SSP, POA&M, audit trail, personnel screening, secure development practice attestation) for me as a vendor inside the client's compliance boundary are out of scope for year 1. CMMC Level 2 includes the contractor's full software supply chain; in those engagements I am inside the boundary, not adjacent to it. That is a different practice than the one I am building. Anti-ICP is updated accordingly.

Compliance fluency as positioning

Most generic AI consultants do not know what DFARS 252.204-7012 is. They sell managed-hosting engagements to federal contractors, the compliance officer kills it silently on review, and they lose the deal without ever understanding why. Knowing the right question to ask in discovery and being able to articulate the three deployment tiers fluently is itself a credibility moat in the GovCon segment. Handled correctly, the compliance reality is a sales asset, not a sales blocker. This framework must be in place before the first GovCon discovery call, which on the launch timeline can be as early as Week 2.

AI usage caps

No retainer includes unlimited AI usage.

Each support plan includes a usage allowance. Usage above the allowance is either:

* passed through at cost,
* passed through at cost plus administrative margin, or
* triggers a plan adjustment.

Usage caps must be based on measured workload as early data comes in. Document workflows can become expensive if volume, OCR, or higher-end models are used heavily.

Initial cap structure for Build #1

These are initial planning assumptions only, based on a back-of-envelope for a single document-extraction workflow processing roughly 50 documents/day with Gemini Flash for extraction and Claude Sonnet for review/drafting. Model pricing, document volume, and workflow shape change quickly. Actual cost caps must be recalculated from the first real workflow's document volume, model mix, OCR needs, and retry rate before Build #1 ships. Do not sign Build #1 with no number at all, and do not rely on these numbers past the first engagement without re-measuring.

Underlying cost model assumptions (initial planning only):

* Extraction: ~10K input / ~1K output tokens per doc on Gemini Flash, 50 docs/day → ~$50–100/month
* Review/drafting: ~5K input / ~2K output tokens on Claude Sonnet, half the docs → ~$300–600/month
* Embeddings, retries, edge cases: 20–30% buffer on top
* Expected total for a moderately busy single-workflow client: $400–900/month

Plan caps (initial planning only — recalculate after Build #1):

* Teardown prototype: included within reason
* Launch Support: $750–$1,200/month cap with explicit pass-through above cap
* Maintenance: $300–$500/month cap with explicit pass-through above cap
* Growth: $1,500–$2,500/month cap, or full pass-through with administrative margin, explicit in SOW

Open-ended language is how solo consultants discover that one client's API bill ate their entire monthly margin. Even a guessed cap with explicit pass-through above is better than no cap. The contract must make this explicit.

Part 8 — Buy vs. Build vs. Integrate

This is a core trust principle.

The Teardown does not assume custom software is always the answer.

Sometimes the right answer is an existing SaaS tool. Sometimes it is a custom workbench. Sometimes it is a thin layer connecting tools they already use.

SaaS is better when

* the workflow is standard
* an existing tool solves 80–90% of the problem
* the tool already integrates with their systems
* the team can adopt it without heavy customization
* pricing is reasonable
* security/compliance posture is already handled
* the workflow does not require company-specific logic

Custom or managed workflow is better when

* the workflow is specific to the business
* documents are inconsistent
* work crosses email, PDFs, spreadsheets, SharePoint/Drive, CRM, and human review
* staff need a custom review queue
* outputs need human approval
* existing tools solve only part of the workflow
* the team has tried tools but the messy middle still remains
* the buyer wants one accountable person to own implementation

Positioning line

I do not compete with SaaS. I make the whole workflow work.

If SaaS is the right answer, I should recommend it. That builds trust and prevents bad custom projects.

Part 16 — Honest Risks and Downsides

1. Founding engagements do not land

If I cannot secure serious founding engagements, the whole plan slows.

Mitigation: Use warm intros first. Do not rely only on cold outreach. Offer founding pricing. Allow free only for high proof value. Create a sample Teardown to show the artifact before proof exists.

2. GovCon niche does not pull

The niche may be more security-sensitive, SaaS-saturated, slow-moving, or skeptical than expected.

Mitigation: Narrow to small/mid-sized contractors first. Avoid enterprise. Avoid classified/highly sensitive workflows. Focus on the messy middle around existing tools. If GovCon shows no traction after 100–200 quality sends and warm-intro attempts, shift to secondary document-heavy operations using the same workbench patterns.

3. SaaS competition wins

Some buyers should use SaaS instead of custom work.

Mitigation: Make buy-vs-build-vs-integrate part of the offer. Do not fight SaaS where it clearly fits. Win where workflows are specific, cross-system, human-reviewed, and poorly served by generic tools.

4. $5k–$10k Teardown price blocks early sales

Early buyers may hesitate because proof is thin.

Mitigation: Founding price at $5k for first 2–3 serious clients. Use credit toward Build. Use honest first-mover-risk framing. Do not drop to bargain pricing unless scope is dramatically reduced.

5. Free pilots create low-commitment clients

Free work can attract low-seriousness clients who do not provide access or testimonial value.

Mitigation: Free only when proof value is high and access is guaranteed in writing. Otherwise founding paid Teardown is preferred.

6. Managed hosting creates operational burden

If I host everything, clients may treat me as always-on critical infrastructure.

Mitigation: Support scope, response times, no 24/7 SLA unless paid, usage caps, liability limits, human-review requirements, monitoring, client-owned-infra option, and clear cancellation/handoff policy.

7. Liability event

A system output could be wrong, delayed, or misused.

Mitigation: Human-in-the-loop design, decision-support framing, audit logs, acceptance criteria, no autonomous high-stakes decisions, E&O insurance, contract liability caps, careful workflow selection.

8. Scope creep destroys margin

Client asks for “one more thing” until the project becomes unprofitable.

Mitigation: One workflow per Teardown. Fixed SOW. UAT classification. Bugs fixed; new features quoted. Launch support includes defined improvement limits.

9. Solo bandwidth crush

Two builds plus support retainers can overwhelm a solo operator.

Mitigation: Hard cap at one major Build at a time until delivery process is proven. Do not sign Build #2 if Build #1 is unstable. Build reusable kit. Prepare subcontractor path by month 6.

10. AI usage costs surprise me

Document extraction and drafting at volume can cost more than expected.

Mitigation: Separate keys/projects per client. Usage monitoring from day one. Contractual caps. Pass-through overage. Optimize model mix.

11. Side projects pull focus

AWD or OmniBid can become avoidance.

Mitigation: AWD inbound-only under 5 hours/week. OmniBid used as sales demo only. No SaaS revival until client data proves a repeated workflow pattern.

12. Income volatility

Project revenue is lumpy.

Mitigation: Keep DOL job until at least 6 months of family expenses in cash and recurring support revenue is meaningful. Prioritize retainers after first Build. Set aside taxes from day one.

13. Market saturation accelerates

AI implementation gets cheaper and more crowded.

Mitigation: Specific niche, trust, document workflow expertise, reusable implementation kit, managed support, and buy-vs-build-vs-integrate positioning.
